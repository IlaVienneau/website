Febrile Seizure Detection

Background

Febrile seizures are a medical phenomenon characterized by rapid, involuntary muscle contractions in patients experiencing a fever. Most common in infants between the age of six months to three years of age, data from the Center for Disease Control and Prevention predicts that there are 80,000 to 200,000 incidents in the United States alone of febrile seizures in infants anually. Currently, there are no portable devices on the market with the capability to detect febrile seizures and protect against their consequences. My sophomore design team and I thus sought to create a device which would meet this need.

Device Design

Our device is designed as a beanie made from breathable fabric, onto which active electrodes are inkjet printed onto an interior elastic headband componenent. These electrodes continuously measure EEG data from the wearer and feed it into a processing unit. If pre-seizure brain state features are detected, this processing unit will trigger inflation of the protective cushions via release of C02 from a canister trigger valve system.

Detection Algorithm

Our algorithm consists of three main components, data inputs, data preprocessing, feature extraction and analysis, and data outputs. Our inputs are recorded EEG signal data from pediatric seizure patients fed into our algorithm as if in real time. We perform preprocessesing to remove both line noise and biological noise using a Butterworth IIR filter and a moving average window, respectively. Thereafter for ten minute data segments, we used band pass filters to extract gamma1 and gamma3 wavelengths from the signal using bandpass filters, and compared the change in spectral power ratio of gamma1 to gamma3 frequency bands to a known value to map the time-period to either a pre-seizure or normal brain state label. In the case of a pre-seizure value indication, we output a signal to trigger cushion inflation.

Protective Cushion

Through literature review, we determined a value for the maximum acceptable force an infant head can withstand. Thereafter determined experimentally that the optimal design specifications for our cushion when inflated are approximately spherical shape with a seven inch radius, and construction from a permeable fabric. These specifications allow optimal impact absorption and decceleration.

Spatiotemporal Pattern Detection

Background

The primary focus of the Keilholz Lab is to characterize the dynamics of neural functional connectivity in both pathogenic and non-pathogenic brain states. Over the course of this semester, I contributed to this mission by writing an algorithm which uses machine learning techniques to extract spatiotemporal patterns of functional network activity from fMRI scans of non-pathogenic human brains in the resting state.

Algorithm Details

The inputs to my algorithm are preprocessed, volumetric fMRI data over time. I prepare this data for use by first applying a mask to the scan for each region of interest (ROI) and averaging the signal value at each time point to extract a sinusoidal time-course for each ROI. These represent the neural activity at those ROIs over time. Thereafter, I transformed the data from the time domain to the spectral domain using a continuous wavelet transform. Thereafter, I isolated relevant data based on the frequencies known to remain in the signal after preprocessing. At this point, I was able to concatenate feature vectors of multiple time points to account for different windows of time, and compared the similarity of these using Kullback Leibler (KL) divergence. For different window lengths, I used this matrix of KL divergences for scans and sets of scans to run a t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding algorithm. To build this algorithm, I adapted the procedure outlined in the journal article “Mapping the stereotyped behavior of freely moving fruit flies” by Gordon J. Berman, Daniel M. Choi, William Bialek, and Joshua W. Shaevitz. Thus far, this algorithm has been used to create a training data set which presents potential patterns in the brain scans used to make it. These patterns can be confirmed and used by the lab in the future as markers for brain pathology.
